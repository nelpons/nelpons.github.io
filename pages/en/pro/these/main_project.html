Multiscale Study of PVDF-Based Nanocomposites for Additive Manufacturing - 2021-2026

This PhD explores the development of electroactive PVDF-based nanocomposites for additive manufacturing, focusing on how processing conditions, microstructure and architecture interact to control mechanical and functional properties.

Approach

Scientific Context

Poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) is a semi-crystalline polymer widely used for its excellent chemical resistance and electroactive properties. However, its behaviour strongly depends on processing conditions and crystalline phase distribution.

The incorporation of nanoclays such as Dellite® 67G offers new opportunities to tailor mechanical properties and induce structural modifications, especially in the context of additive manufacturing.

Objectives

  • Understand the influence of nanoclay content on PVDF structure and properties
  • Investigate the effect of 3D printing orientation on mechanical behaviour
  • Correlate microstructure with macroscopic mechanical response
  • Analyse deformation mechanisms under tensile loading

Methodology

A multiscale experimental approach was developed combining materials processing, mechanical testing and advanced characterisation techniques.

  • Extrusion and filament preparation
  • Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) 3D printing
  • Tensile testing with in-situ measurements
  • Synchrotron X-ray diffraction during deformation
  • Infrared thermography monitoring

Key Results

  • Strong anisotropy induced by printing orientation, directly impacting mechanical performance
  • Nanoclay addition modifies crystalline phases and enhances electroactive behaviour
  • Distinct deformation mechanisms identified depending on composition and architecture
  • In situ experiments reveal limited coupling between damage and crystalline reorganisation

Perspectives

This work opens perspectives for optimising PVDF-based materials for functional applications, particularly where anisotropy and electroactive properties are critical. Future work could focus on scaling up processes and exploring other nanofillers. This work contributes to the development of functional materials for sensors, energy harvesting, and smart structures, where controlled electroactive properties are critical.
